Executive summary

Milano Gelato Cube is a 10 m² walk-up cube near Porta Romana or Isola, serving exactly 12 gelato flavours at any given moment — rotated weekly, never more, never less. The constraint is the product: twelve is what fits in one Bravo Trittico batch freezer per day, and the discipline keeps every flavour fresh-churned within 24 hours.

No seating, no coffee, no pastries, no sorbet alongside gelato — just 12 gelati, three sizes, one line. Ticket €4.20 average. 300 scoops/day sustainable at summer peak, 120/day in winter, weighted average 200/day.

The concept

Why twelve

A Bravo Trittico 305P batch freezer makes 12 flavours/day with two hours of active churning. Twelve is also what fits legibly on a single chalkboard. Twelve is what a customer can read, decide, and order in under 90 seconds — which is what a walk-up window needs.

Three commitments

  • 12 flavours, never 11, never 13 — the chalkboard is the discipline
  • Rotate on Monday morning — 4 permanent, 8 rotating
  • Whatever isn't sold by closing goes home with the team or to Banco Alimentare — never to tomorrow

What it is not

  • Not a gelateria — no seating, no tables, no table service
  • Not a cafeteria — no espresso, no pastries, no granita
  • Not a supermarket — no take-home tubs, no freezer bags
  • Not a chain — one cube, in Milan, forever

The four permanent

01 Pistacchio di Bronte DOP
02 Stracciatella fiordilatte
03 Nocciola Piemonte IGP
04 Cioccolato fondente 72%

The other eight rotate weekly based on market, season and mood.

Target audience

Primary

The Milano local, 28–50

Lives within 500m, walks past 3–4 times/week. Comes for the rotation — "what's new this week" is the primary hook. Average ticket €4.20, 2 visits/week in summer, 1/week in winter.

~60% of revenue

Secondary

The lunch-break office crowd, 25–45

Works nearby, eats gelato instead of dessert after lunch. Mon–Fri 13:00–14:30 peak. Predictable volume, high repeat.

~25% of revenue

Tertiary

The weekend visitor, 22–55

Turin, Bergamo, Como weekenders. Follows Instagram for flavour announcements. Arrives Saturday afternoon. Instagrammable but never the point.

~15% of revenue

What they are NOT

Tourists in Milano for 24 hours looking for "best gelato in city" on TripAdvisor. Tour groups. Families looking for a sit-down scoop-and-spoon experience. Children under 6 (the line is too fast, the decision window too short).

Unique value proposition

The five USPs

  1. Exactly twelve. Never more. The constraint is the story.
  2. Churned today. Every cone from a batch made within 24 hours.
  3. Weekly rotation. Monday = new eight. Regulars get a ritual.
  4. Zero-waste discipline. Unsold gelato leaves the shop daily.
  5. Walk-up cube. No tables means no friction, no cleanup, no turnover drama.

Why these five survive

Italian gelato is a crowded market dominated by two archetypes: the tourist trap (50 flavours, neon colours, mountains of pile) and the old-school family gelateria (8 flavours, 30 years, no social media). Milano Gelato Cube takes the family discipline and adds the tourist's visibility — twelve flavours is enough to be interesting, few enough to be trustable.

The moat is the Monday ritual. A customer learns the rotation rhythm in three weeks and starts planning their week around it. No competitor with a 30-flavour cabinet can replicate that cadence.

Positioning statement

For Milanesi who treat gelato as a weekly ritual rather than an occasional treat, Milano Gelato Cube is the 10 m² walk-up window whose 12-flavour weekly rotation turns a scoop into a standing appointment with Monday morning.

Equipment · detail

Bravo Trittico 305P · the heart

Italian-made combined pasteuriser + batch freezer + heat-treatment machine in one 305L chamber. One machine does the entire production cycle: heats the base to 85°C, cools, stores, and churns on demand. 12 flavours per day is its sweet spot — this is the entire reason the cube exists at 12.

Power: 9 kW · Duty cycle: 2h/day active

Ifi Tonda 12 · the window

Round 12-pan display cabinet from Brescia manufacturer Ifi. Pozzetti-style (lidded, not open cabinet), which keeps gelato at −12°C stable and invisible from the window until the customer points. The "show me" moment is a ritual — lid comes off, cold vapour rises, customer sees.

Italian-made, 48-month warranty, 2-week lead time

Zeroll scoops · the tool

Aluminium-alloy scoop filled with conductive fluid that draws heat from the user's hand to release gelato cleanly. Invented in Fort Wayne in 1935, still the global standard. Three size-16 (standard scoop) and three size-20 (piccolo) scoops, rotating to stay warm-handed.

Never dishwasher — hand-wash only. Lifetime tool.

Hagoromo chalk · the discipline

Legendary Japanese chalk, discontinued in 2015 then revived by a Korean manufacturer. Twelve colours. The act of rewriting the chalkboard every Monday morning is the brand ritual — it cannot be printed, it cannot be digital, it must be chalk.

~€60 / year at current volume

  • No jewellery visible (rings, watches, bracelets)
  • No perfume or cologne
  • No visible phones during service

Apron spec

Sourced from a Milanese workshop in Porta Genova. Heavy-weight canvas, 400 gsm, natural cream. No logo. Replaced every 3 months.

Why this matters

The staff uniform is neutral on purpose — the cabinet and the chalkboard should be the only things the customer looks at. An over-branded uniform competes with the product for attention.

Operating principles

  1. Twelve. Always twelve. Never eleven, never thirteen, never "special flavour today".
  2. Monday is rewrite day. Chalkboard updates at 08:00, eight new rotating flavours.
  3. Churned within 24 hours. If it wasn't made yesterday or today, it doesn't get served.
  4. Zero waste overnight. Unsold gelato leaves with staff or to Banco Alimentare — never back into cabinet.
  5. No seating, ever. The cube is walk-up. Adding benches kills turnover and opens HACCP exposure.
  6. No coffee, no pastry. One product category, executed completely.
  7. DOP where DOP exists. Pistacchio di Bronte, Nocciola Piemonte IGP, Fragola di Nemi — non-negotiable.
  8. Summer funds winter. Jun–Aug profits cover Dec–Feb losses. Cash management is the game.
  9. The chalkboard is the brand. Not the logo, not the signage — the twelve hand-written names.
  10. Close Tuesdays. One day of rest for Bravo Trittico, one day for the team.
